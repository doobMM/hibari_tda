% ── Abstract ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
We present a computational pipeline that extracts the topological structure
of a musical composition via persistent homology and re-synthesizes new
musical pieces that preserve that structure. Applied to Ryuichi Sakamoto's
\emph{hibari} (from the 2009 album \emph{out of noise}), the pipeline (i)
constructs a weighted note-network from the MIDI quantization, (ii) computes
$H_1$ persistent homology under four different distance functions
(frequency, Tonnetz, voice-leading, DFT), (iii) builds an overlap matrix
recording cycle activations across the timeline, and (iv) generates new
music via either probabilistic sampling (Algorithm~1) or deep learning
(Algorithm~2; FC / LSTM / Transformer).
Over $N=20$ independent trials per configuration, the Tonnetz distance
reduces the pitch Jensen--Shannon divergence from $0.0753 \pm 0.0033$
(frequency baseline) to $0.0398 \pm 0.0031$, a $47\%$ improvement
(Welch $t = 35.1$, $p < 10^{-20}$, Cohen's $d = 11.1$). Surprisingly,
the simplest FC model outperforms LSTM and Transformer, which we
interpret as an artifact of \emph{hibari}'s aesthetic design that
privileges spatial placement of notes over temporal causality.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Persistent homology, Topological data analysis, Music generation,
Tonnetz, Jensen--Shannon divergence, Multi-label classification,
Vietoris--Rips complex.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

% ── Section I: Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Topological Data Analysis (TDA) offers a principled way to extract
geometric structure from data that is robust to noise and scale
\cite{carlsson2009,edelsbrunner2010}. In this work, we apply TDA to
a specific musical composition---Ryuichi Sakamoto's \emph{hibari}---and
ask whether the topological invariants (specifically, the first
persistent homology group $H_1$) can serve as a structural seed for
generating new, stylistically faithful music.

\textbf{Motivation.} Existing structure-preserving music generation
approaches either rely on symbolic grammars or on deep generative models
trained on large corpora. Neither approach cleanly separates
``what structure is preserved'' from ``how new tokens are produced.''
Persistent homology provides such a separation: the cycles discovered
in the note-network are a compact invariant, and the overlap matrix
tracking their temporal activation serves as a decoupled seed for
downstream generation.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item A four-stage pipeline (preprocessing, homology, overlap, generation)
      with two alternative generators (probabilistic vs neural).
\item A systematic comparison of four distance functions
      (frequency, Tonnetz, voice-leading, DFT) with $N=20$ statistical
      repetitions, establishing Tonnetz as the strongest choice for
      \emph{hibari}.
\item An ablation over cycle subset size $K \in \{10, 17, 46\}$
      demonstrating monotone improvement with diminishing returns.
\item A qualitative interpretation of the FC model's unexpected dominance
      over sequence models, situated in the aesthetic context of the
      \emph{out of noise} album.
\end{enumerate}

% ── Section II: Background ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Mathematical Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Vietoris--Rips Complex and Persistent Homology}
\label{subsec:vr}

Given a finite metric space $(X, d)$ and a scale parameter
$\varepsilon \ge 0$, the Vietoris--Rips complex
$\mathrm{VR}_\varepsilon(X)$ is the simplicial complex whose simplices
are subsets $\sigma \subseteq X$ with pairwise distances at most
$\varepsilon$. As $\varepsilon$ increases from $0$, the resulting
family $\{\mathrm{VR}_{\varepsilon_i}\}_{i=0}^{m}$ forms a filtration,
and persistent homology tracks the birth and death of homology classes
along this filtration \cite{edelsbrunner2010}.

\subsection{Simplicial Homology}
\label{subsec:simplicial}

For an oriented simplicial complex $K$ and a field $\mathbb{F}_2$,
the chain group $C_n(K)$ is freely generated by the $n$-simplices.
The boundary map $\partial_n : C_n \to C_{n-1}$ sends each simplex to
the formal sum of its faces. The $n$-th homology group is
\begin{equation}
H_n(K) = \ker \partial_n \,/\, \mathrm{im}\, \partial_{n+1}.
\end{equation}
In this paper, we focus on $H_1$, whose generators correspond to
topological cycles in the note-network.

\subsection{Musical Distance Functions}
\label{subsec:distances}

We compare four distance functions between notes
$n_i = (p_i, d_i)$ (pitch, duration):

\paragraph{Frequency distance (baseline)} $d_{\mathrm{freq}}(n_i, n_j) =
1 / w(n_i, n_j)$, where $w$ is the adjacency frequency.

\paragraph{Tonnetz distance} Pitch classes are embedded in a hexagonal
lattice with neighbors at $\pm 3, \pm 4, \pm 7$ semitones
(minor 3rd, major 3rd, perfect 5th). The distance between two pitch
classes is the shortest path in this graph, pre-computed via
breadth-first search into a $12 \times 12$ table \cite{tymoczko2012}.

\paragraph{Voice-leading distance} The unsigned semitone difference
$|p_i - p_j|$ plus a duration term.

\paragraph{DFT distance} Pitch classes are mapped to indicator
vectors in $\mathbb{R}^{12}$; the Euclidean distance between the
magnitudes of their first six Fourier coefficients defines the
distance \cite{tymoczko2008}.

\subsection{Overlap Matrix}

Let $\{c_1, \ldots, c_K\}$ be the cycles discovered under a given
distance function, and let $V(c)$ denote the vertex (note-label) set
of cycle $c$. The \emph{overlap matrix}
$O \in \{0,1\}^{T \times K}$ is defined by
\begin{equation}
O[t, c] = \mathbb{1}\Big[\,|V(c) \cap A_t| \ge \tau \cdot |V(c)|\,\Big],
\end{equation}
where $A_t$ is the set of notes active at time $t$ and $\tau = 0.35$
is the threshold. This $O$ is the seed consumed by both generators.

\subsection{Jensen--Shannon Divergence}

For two discrete distributions $P, Q$, the Jensen--Shannon divergence
is the symmetrized Kullback--Leibler divergence
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{JS}}(P \,\|\, Q) = \frac{1}{2} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \,\|\, M) +
                              \frac{1}{2} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(Q \,\|\, M),
\end{equation}
where $M = (P + Q)/2$. It is bounded above by $\log 2 \approx 0.693$,
achieved when $P, Q$ have disjoint support.

\section{Pipeline Overview}
\label{sec:pipeline}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} summarizes the four stages of our pipeline.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{fig1_pipeline.png}
\caption{Four-stage pipeline: preprocessing, persistent homology,
overlap matrix construction, and generation.}

\subsection{Stage 1 --- Preprocessing}
The MIDI file is quantized to 8th-note units ($T = 1088$ timepoints).
The two instruments are separated, and each unique (pitch, duration)
pair is assigned a 1-indexed label. For \emph{hibari}, this yields
$N = 23$ distinct notes and $C = 17$ distinct chord labels.

\subsection{Stage 2 --- Persistent Homology}
A weighted note-network is constructed from the chord sequence, and
a distance matrix is derived via the chosen distance function. The
Vietoris--Rips filtration and $H_1$ computation are performed over a
grid of rate parameters $r_t \in [0, 1.5]$, using the
\texttt{professor.py} reference implementation validated against
\texttt{ripser} \cite{bauer2021}.

\subsection{Stage 3 --- Overlap Matrix}
For each discovered cycle $c$, an activation row is produced for
every timepoint and thresholded to obtain the binary overlap matrix.

\subsection{Stage 4 --- Generation}
Either Algorithm~1 (probabilistic sampling, Section~\ref{subsec:algo1})
or Algorithm~2 (neural, Section~\ref{subsec:algo2}) consumes the
overlap matrix and outputs a MusicXML-compatible note list.

% ── Section IV: Algorithms ──────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Generation Algorithms}
\label{sec:algorithms}

\subsection{Algorithm 1 --- Topological Sampling}
\label{subsec:algo1}

For each timepoint $t$, if any cycle is active
($\sum_c O[t,c] > 0$), a note is sampled uniformly from the
\emph{intersection} of vertex sets of the active cycles. Otherwise,
a note is sampled from the complement of the \emph{union} of
vertex sets of neighboring active cycles, to avoid topological
``bleeding.'' Collisions in note placement trigger up to
$R = 50$ re-sampling attempts.

\subsection{Algorithm 2 --- Neural Sequence Models}
\label{subsec:algo2}

We train three architectures on the mapping
$f_\theta : O[t,:] \mapsto y[t,:]$, where $y$ is the multi-hot
note vector of the original piece at time $t$:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{FC} --- a two-layer fully connected network with
      hidden dimension $H = 128$, processing each timepoint
      independently.
\item \textbf{LSTM} --- a 2-layer LSTM capturing forward sequential
      context.
\item \textbf{Transformer} --- a 2-layer, 4-head Transformer encoder
      with learned positional embeddings.
\end{itemize}
All models are trained with \texttt{BCEWithLogitsLoss} (multi-label
binary cross-entropy with integrated sigmoid) and data augmentation
via cycle-subset sampling, circular time-axis shifting, and bit-flip
noise injection. At inference time, an \emph{adaptive threshold}
$\theta = \mathrm{quantile}(P, 1 - \rho_{\mathrm{on}})$ matches the
original piece's ON ratio $\rho_{\mathrm{on}} \approx 0.15$.

% ── Section V: Experiments ──────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
All experiments use the same chord-height pattern, the same random seed
scheme, and $N = 20$ independent repetitions per configuration. The
main metric is pitch JS divergence against the original; note coverage
and KL divergence are reported as auxiliary signals. All results are
stored in \texttt{docs/step3\_data/step3\_results.json}.

\subsection{Distance Function Baselines}

Table~\ref{tab:baselines} reports the JS divergence achieved by
Algorithm~1 under each of the four distance functions. The Tonnetz
distance is strongest, reducing the baseline (frequency) JS divergence
by roughly $47\%$.

\subsection{Cycle Subset Ablation}

Fixing the Tonnetz distance, we vary the cycle count
$K \in \{10, 17, 46\}$. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, JS
divergence decreases monotonically with $K$, but with diminishing
returns --- the gain from $K: 17 \to 46$ ($\Delta = 0.034$ for 29
additional cycles) is only marginally larger than that from
$K: 10 \to 17$ ($\Delta = 0.025$ for 7 additional cycles).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Cycle subset ablation (Tonnetz, $N=20$).}
\label{tab:ablation}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
$K$ & JS (mean $\pm$ std) & Note Coverage \\
\midrule
10  & $0.0991 \pm 0.0038$ & $0.980$ \\
17  & $0.0740 \pm 0.0038$ & $0.996$ \\
46  & $\mathbf{0.0397 \pm 0.0025}$ & $\mathbf{1.000}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Statistical Significance}

Applying Welch's $t$-test to the frequency-vs-Tonnetz comparison:
\begin{equation}
t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}
         {\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}}
  \approx 35.1,
\end{equation}
with Welch--Satterthwaite degrees of freedom $\nu \approx 38$, giving
$p < 10^{-20}$. Cohen's effect size is $d \approx 11.1$, an order of
magnitude beyond the conventional ``large effect'' threshold of $0.8$.
We use Welch's variant (rather than Student's) because the equal-variance
assumption is not justified a priori.

\subsection{Deep Learning Model Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:dl} summarizes the results from a single-run
hyperparameter grid search over 30 configurations
(FC / LSTM / Transformer $\times$ hidden dim $\times$ learning rate
$\times$ dropout $\times$ augmentation). The simplest FC model
achieves the lowest JS divergence ($0.0015$). We interpret this as
consistent with the aesthetic of \emph{hibari}'s parent album,
\emph{out of noise}, which deliberately blurs the line between noise
and melody by emphasizing spatial note placement over temporal
causality. A model that ignores time context (FC) thus aligns
better with the compositional design than one that imposes it
(LSTM, Transformer).

% ── Section VI: Discussion ──────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Music-theoretic distance matters.} The $47\%$ reduction in JS
divergence from frequency to Tonnetz confirms that encoding
music-theoretic structure into the distance function changes the
topological invariants that are discovered, and these invariants
translate into better generation.

\textbf{Diminishing returns of cycle count.} Tripling the cycle set
($K: 17 \to 46$) only improves JS divergence by a factor comparable
to adding seven cycles ($K: 10 \to 17$). This justifies the
greedy-forward-selection strategy (Section~\ref{subsec:vr}).

\textbf{Aesthetics constrain model choice.} The dominance of FC over
sequence models is not a universal property of music generation;
rather, it reflects \emph{hibari}'s non-sequential compositional
character. A corpus whose melody depends on time causality would
likely invert this ranking.

\textbf{Future work.} (i) Re-running DL experiments with $N \ge 5$
repetitions; (ii) greedy-selected subsets in the ablation study;
(iii) grid search over Tonnetz hybrid's $\alpha$ parameter;
(iv) generalization to other pieces from \emph{out of noise} and
to corpora with stronger temporal dependencies.
